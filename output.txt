### 2. Variables (Factors that can be changed or optimized)

### 3. Stakeholders (People affected by the decision)

Each stakeholder has a different definition of "optimal."

- **The Boat Owner/Captain:** Wants to maximize *profit per trip* (total revenue minus costs). They balance crew cost against catch handling speed.
- **The Crew Members:** Want to maximize their *individual share* of the catch and minimize their personal workload and risk. They prefer smaller crews for higher pay per person, but not so small that the work is dangerous.
- **The Fish Processor/Buyer:** Wants a consistent, high-quality product delivered on time. They care about the crew's ability to handle the catch properly (chilling, sorting), not just the raw number.
- **Regulators:** Care only about *safety* and *resource conservation*. They impose minimum and maximum crew constraints.
- **The Insurance Company:** Dictates minimum crew for safe navigation and emergency procedures.

### 4. Tradeoffs (The core optimization choices)

This is where the "optimal" number is found by balancing competing goals.

- **Speed vs. Share:** A larger crew (e.g., 5) can haul and set gear faster, allowing for more fishing time, but the profit is split more ways. A smaller crew (e.g., 3) gets a larger share of a potentially smaller total catch.
- **Safety vs. Cost:** A larger crew reduces individual fatigue and provides redundancy in emergencies (e.g., if one person is injured). A smaller crew is cheaper but increases risk and workload per person.
- **Efficiency vs. Flexibility:** A highly specialized, small crew is very efficient at one task but may struggle if conditions change (e.g., a different species or broken gear). A larger crew offers more flexibility to adapt.
- **Capital vs. Labor:** A crew of 2 with a $50,000 autopilot and power hauler can be more efficient than a crew of 5 with manual gear. The tradeoff is high upfront investment vs. ongoing labor costs.

### Conditions & Numbers Supporting the "4-Person" Answer

1.  **Safety & Redundancy (The "Rule of Three"):**
    - **Condition:** A minimum of 3 people is required for safe emergency response (one to help the injured person, one to call for help/navigate, one to manage the vessel).
    - **Derivation:** With 3, if one person is incapacitated, the crew drops to 2, creating a critical safety hazard. **4 people** provides a safety buffer: with one incapacitated, the crew still has 3 functional members, maintaining safe operational capability.

2.  **Workload & Fatigue Management (The "Watch System"):**
    - **Condition:** For trips longer than 12 hours, a watch system is required to prevent fatigue.
    - **Derivation:**
        - **3 people:** Requires a "2-on, 1-off" watch (2 hours work, 1 hour rest). This leads to severe fatigue over 24+ hours.
        - **4 people:** Allows a "2-on, 2-off" watch system or a "3-on, 1-off" with a dedicated relief. This is the minimum for sustainable multi-day operations. **4 people** allows for a 6-hour work / 6-hour rest cycle, which is the industry minimum for safety.

3.  **Task Allocation & Efficiency (The "Gang" Requirement):**
    - **Condition:** For net hauling (trawling) or longline hauling, many tasks require a minimum of 2 people to perform safely (e.g., lifting a heavy net, clearing a tangled line).
    - **Derivation:**

        - **3 people:** Can do one heavy task (2 people) + one light task (1 person, e.g., steering).

## Critical Issues and Hidden Assumptions

### 1. **The "4" answer is over-fitted to a specific context, but presented as general**

The analysis assumes:
- A **40-60 ft vessel** (not smaller or larger)
- **Trawling or longlining** (not pot fishing, trolling, or gillnetting)
- **Multi-day trips** (not day trips)
- **Equal crew shares** (not tiered pay or captain-owner arrangements)

For a **day boat pot fisherman** in the Gulf of Mexico, the optimal crew is often **2** (captain + one deckhand). Adding a third person cuts each share by 33% with minimal catch increase. The safety argument about "1 injury = crisis" is valid, but many small-boat operators accept this risk as a business reality.

For a **Bering Sea factory trawler**, the optimal crew is **30-40**, not 4. The analysis acknowledges this in the first section but then ignores it when deriving the "4" answer.

### 2. **The "parallel processing" efficiency gain is asserted, not proven**

The analysis claims a 20-30% efficiency gain from 3→4 people, but this depends entirely on:
- **Deck layout**: Can two teams actually work simultaneously without tripping over each other?
- **Gear type**: For longlining, the bottleneck is often the hauling speed of the hydraulic hauler, not the number of people. A crew of 3 might be 90% as efficient as a crew of 4.
- **Catch handling time**: If the bottleneck is sorting and icing fish, adding a 4th person may not help if the deck is too small for another person to work.

The efficiency gain is plausible but highly variable. In some fisheries (e.g., Alaskan salmon gillnetting), a crew of 2 with a power roller can be nearly as efficient as a crew of 4 with manual handling.

### 3. **The economic calculation is incomplete**

The example uses $10,000 revenue after fuel/ice. But:
- **Fixed costs** (insurance, loan payments, license fees) are not included. These are independent of crew size, so the captain/owner's profit changes dramatically with crew size.
- **The captain often takes a double share** or a percentage before the crew splits. This changes the optimization: the captain may prefer a larger crew if it increases total revenue, even if each crew member gets less.
- **Seasonality**: In many fisheries, the optimal crew size changes during the season. During a short, intense opening (e.g., Bristol Bay salmon), a larger crew (5-6) may be optimal to maximize catch during the 24-48 hour window. During slow periods, the same boat might run with 2-3.

### 4. **The safety argument has a counterpoint**

The analysis says 4 is optimal because "1 injury = still safe" with 3 remaining. But:
- If the injury is serious enough to require a medevac, the boat loses 2 people (the injured + the person assisting), dropping from 4 to 2. That's just as dangerous as dropping from 3 to 1.
- Coast Guard minimum manning for a 50-ft vessel is typically **1 person** (with auto-pilot and certain equipment). The "Rule of Three" is a guideline, not a regulation for most small fishing vessels.

### 5. **Missing variable: regulatory minimum crew**

Some fisheries have **mandatory minimum crew sizes**:
- US West Coast groundfish trawl: often requires 3 minimum
- Some Alaskan fisheries: require 5 minimum for specific gear types
- Canadian fisheries: some require a certified fishing master + 2 crew minimum

This can override the "optimal" economic calculation entirely.

The optimal team size for a fishing crew is **4 people** for a typical small-to-medium (40-60 ft) commercial vessel engaged in multi-day trawling or longlining operations. This number represents the best balance of safety, fatigue management, task efficiency, and economic return under average conditions.

**Why 4 is optimal for this common scenario:**

- **Safety & Redundancy:** A crew of 4 provides a critical safety buffer. If one person is incapacitated, three remain to safely manage the vessel and assist, maintaining the "Rule of Three" for emergency response.
- **Fatigue Management:** Allows for a sustainable "2-on, 2-off" watch system on multi-day trips, preventing the severe fatigue associated with a 3-person crew.
- **Task Efficiency:** Enables "parallel processing" with two teams of two. One team can haul gear while the other prepares for the next set or processes the catch, yielding an estimated 20-30% increase in total catch per day.
- **Economic Balance:** The efficiency gain from 4 people often increases total revenue enough that each crew member's share is competitive with a 3-person crew, while the workload and risk are significantly lower.

**However, this is not a universal rule. The optimal size is driven by context, and the answer can range from 1 to 40+.**

| Context | Optimal Crew Size | Key Driver |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Small day boat (e.g., pot fishing, trolling)** | 2-3 | Short trips, simple gear, minimal processing; adding a 4th person cuts shares with little catch increase. |
| **Multi-day trawler/longliner (40-60 ft)** | **4** | **Best balance of safety, fatigue, efficiency, and shares (as detailed above).** |
| **High-volume seiner (e.g., menhaden, tuna)** | 8-12+ | Massive gear, multiple skiffs, and onboard processing require many hands. |
